# Mukoko Manifesto — Amendment: Ubuntu, Structurally

> This amendment is a supersession-in-place. Prior Manifesto versions
> (including `MUKOKO_MANIFESTO_v4_0_2.md`) are **not** modified. Where this
> amendment overlaps with an earlier statement, the amendment wins.

**Status:** Adopted alongside the Ubuntu Five Pillars and Five Principles wire-up (design-portal issue #45, 2026-04). The structural model below sits underneath the existing Five Ubuntu Questions — it does not replace them.

---

## § Ubuntu, Structurally

The Mukoko Manifesto has, until now, expressed Ubuntu only through the **Five Ubuntu Questions** — a decision filter that evaluates whether a product change should ship. The Questions are necessary but not sufficient. A filter without an underlying model is just an opinion check: it cannot say _why_ a decision strengthens community or _which sphere_ of relationship it touches.

This amendment introduces the structural model that sits beneath the Questions. Two five-fold sets, both derived from published African scholarship, both already populated as canonical tables in the Mukoko platform's source-of-truth database:

- **Five Pillars** — _where Ubuntu is situated_. The spheres of relationship in which a person lives Ubuntu: family, community, society, environment, spirituality. The Pillars are the **context** Ubuntu acts within.
- **Five Principles** — _how Ubuntu is embodied_. The behavioural virtues that translate Ubuntu into action: survival, solidarity, compassion, respect, dignity. The Principles are the **conduct** Ubuntu requires.
- **Five Questions** — _what Ubuntu evaluates_. The decision filter applied to every product change. The Questions are the **gate** every shipping increment must clear.

Together they form a complete loop. The Pillars name where we are. The Principles name how we should act there. The Questions ask whether the next change honours both.

Ubuntu does not live at one layer of the platform — it sits underneath every layer. It is the philosophical substrate of the architecture, not a layer inside it. The `layers_affected` array on this amendment is intentionally empty.

## § Scholarly provenance

The derivation is sovereign. Both sets cite African scholarship; the lead source for the Principles, Lovemore Mbigi, was Zimbabwean (died in Harare, June 2023).

- **Pillars** — Mugumbate, J., Mupedziswa, R., Twikirize, J., Mthethwa, E., Desta, A., & Oyinlola, O. (2023). _Understanding Ubuntu and its contribution to social work education in Africa and other regions of the world._ Social Work Education, 43(4), 1123–1139.
- **Principles** — Mbigi, L. (1997). _Ubuntu: The African Dream in Management._ Pretoria: Sigma Press. The Collective Fingers Theory — _chara chimwe hachitswanyi inda_, one finger cannot crush a louse.
- Samkange, S. & Samkange, T.M. (1980). _Hunhuism or Ubuntuism: A Zimbabwe Indigenous Political Philosophy._ Salisbury: Graham Publishing.
- Ramose, M. (2002). _The Philosophy of Ubuntu as a Philosophy._ Oxford University Press.
- Poovan, N. et al. (2006) and Du Toit, Poovan & Engelbretch (2006) — additional attribution for respect/dignity and compassion.

The platform must cite these sources, not invent alternatives. In particular, the platform must not promote later, geographically displaced re-readings of Ubuntu (especially the U.S. juvenile-justice "restorative justice" frame) as if they were equivalent.

**Explicit exclusion.** Restorative justice is not one of the five principles. It is a modern American framework developed in juvenile justice and therapeutic discourse, not an African one. Ubuntu addresses harm through the continuous practice of the virtues above, not through a separate remedial process. The community does not define itself by how it handles wrongdoing — it defines itself by how it lives.

## § Mathematical resonance with the Order

The Mukoko Order names five as the number of ethics — the instrument through which intention becomes action. Before this amendment, Ubuntu contributed one five-fold structure to the ethics domain (the Questions). With this amendment, Ubuntu contributes three: **Questions** (filter), **Pillars** (context), **Principles** (conduct).

Two hands. Ten fingers. One grip.

That symmetry is not decoration. The Mbigi source itself uses the hand as metaphor — Collective Fingers Theory: a single finger cannot crush a louse; only fingers acting together can. The structural model of Ubuntu in this platform is itself a Collective Fingers structure: five Pillars on one hand, five Principles on the other, working in concert.

No locked count changes. Seven covenants stay seven. Seventeen mini-apps stay seventeen. Twelve manifesto sections stay twelve. Five remains the number of ethics. The amendment adds two more fives to the ethics domain; it does not modify any existing count.

## § Closing Shona

_Chara chimwe hachitswanyi inda._

One finger cannot crush a louse. The Manifesto is a single document, but the platform it describes is a community of acts — and no single act, however large, holds the platform up alone. The Pillars hold; the Principles act; the Questions ask. Together they are Ubuntu.

_Ndiri nekuti tiri._ I am because we are.
